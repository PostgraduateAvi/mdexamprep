Primary Hemostasis
Platelet plug formation — the first response to vascular injury
1

Vascular Injury

Endothelial damage exposes subendothelial collagen

2

Platelet Adhesion

vWF bridges platelets to exposed collagen via GPIb receptor

vWF acts as molecular glue between collagen and platelet GPIb
3

Platelet Activation

Shape change from disc to spiny sphere; granule release

Releases ADP, TxA2, serotonin — recruits more platelets
4

Platelet Aggregation

GPIIb/IIIa receptors cross-linked by fibrinogen bridges

Fibrinogen binds GPIIb/IIIa on adjacent platelets
5

Platelet Plug

Primary hemostatic plug formed — temporary seal

Requires secondary hemostasis (fibrin) for stabilisation

Drugs That Interfere

Aspirin — inhibits COX, blocks TxA2 synthesis
Clopidogrel — blocks P2Y12 (ADP receptor) on platelets
Abciximab, Eptifibatide — GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors, block aggregation

Intrinsic Pathway — monitored by aPTT

XI → XIa Activated by XIIa
IX → IXa Activated by XIa
IXa + VIIIa → Tenase complex → activates Factor X (VIIIa = cofactor)

TF Tissue Factor released from damaged cells
TF + VIIa → Activates Factor X
Common Pathway — both PT and aPTT
Xa + Va → Prothrombinase complex (Va = cofactor)
II → IIa Prothrombin → Thrombin
I → Ia Fibrinogen → Fibrin
XIIIa → Cross-links fibrin → Stable clot

Vitamin K-Dependent Factors

Factors II, VII, IX, X + Protein C, Protein S

Mnemonic: "1972" → factors 10, 9, 7, 2
Fibrinolysis
Clot breakdown — restoring vessel patency
Plasminogen → Plasmin → Fibrin degradation → FDPs + D-dimer
Plasminogen → Plasmin via tPA (tissue plasminogen activator)

Thrombolytics (activate fibrinolysis)

Streptokinase — binds plasminogen, forms activator complex
Alteplase (tPA) — directly converts plasminogen to plasmin; fibrin-selective
Tenecteplase — modified tPA, longer half-life, single bolus

Antifibrinolytics (inhibit fibrinolysis)

Tranexamic acid — blocks plasminogen → plasmin conversion
Aminocaproic acid — same mechanism, used in cardiac surgery / trauma

Both bind lysine-binding sites on plasminogen, preventing fibrin binding

DIC Pathophysiology
Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation

Triggers

Sepsis, malignancy, obstetric complications (abruption, amniotic fluid embolism), massive trauma, snake bites

Trigger — systemic release of tissue factor / procoagulants
↓
Widespread coagulation activation — microthrombi in small vessels
↓
Consumption — clotting factors + platelets used up
↓
Paradox — simultaneous thrombosis AND hemorrhage

8 Warfarin-induced skin necrosis on day 3 of therapy
